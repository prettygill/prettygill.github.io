Case File Opens

It was raining the day they called me in. The kind of rain that perfectly sets the scene for a crime mystery.

Hello everyone, let me introduce myself. My name is Mark. Better known as Detective Mark. I work in the deep depths of the human body — the stem cell districts, the quiet alleys where cells work to divide day and night to keep the body running. Around thirty-seven trillion human cell residents reside in this metropolis of skin, each with their own jobs to do and following the same rules. Most of them cooperate, divide when they're told to divide, die when they're told to die, keep to their districts. A beautiful, brutal social contract that's been running for the last 600 million years.

Most of the cells follow the rules.

Only, most of them..

The case that I'm about to walk you guys through is perhaps one of the oldest in the books. It predates nations, whole family lineages, even fossils. Multicellular life has existed for 600 million years and this same crime repeatedly keeps on showing up at the scene.

Cancer.

The evidence trail goes far deeper than even human history. Tumours have been discovered in fossilised bones going back millions of years. Paleontologists have even found signs of cancer in dinosaurs as their vertebrae were marked by growths that were abnormal and preserved in stone (Rothschild et al., 2003).

Mark — on the peculiar nature of this case The strange part of this case is that it isn't a guessing game. We aren't trying to find the culprit. We already know who it is. We've known the suspect for a long time. The culprit just so happens to be one of our own cells.

The real question, the one that keeps on landing on my desk in a case file, is why this crime keeps on occurring and happening at all. Why even after hundreds of million of years of natural selection, evolution has never eliminated the possibility of cancer?

Yet however the case has changed over time. Cancer has affected humans since prehistoric times, however the prevalence has increased dramatically in recent decades. Part of the explanation for this is simple, people live longer. As the longer the body runs, the more opportunities the cells have to accumulate mutations. More aging populations means more time for the crime to unfold (Hulvat, 2020). BUT however, that is not the whole story.

In the past half century, the number of case files has multiplied along with changes associated to human behaviour and the environment. New layers have been added to the investigation due to increased exposure from carcinogens, from pollution to chemicals found in consumer products (Franken et al., 2017). Still, these factors only help to explain part of the picture.

What every textbook gets wrong about this case. They treat cancer like an accident, a random error, a molecule misfiring, or just like its bad luck in a dark alley. There is a factor of truth to that but it misses the deeper story. The deeper story is that the cell that becomes cancer was built to do almost everything it ends up doing. Divide without stopping. Resist death. Renew itself repeatedly. These aren't criminal acts it learned on the street. They're its original programming. The programming that keeps you alive.

🔬 Stem Cells — The Body's Maintenance Crew Your intestinal lining replaces itself around every five days. Stem cells handle that. Your bone marrow produces around two hundred billion red blood cells every day. Stem cells handle that too. Skin, hair follicles, immune cells, all are stem cells. They divide fast, live long, and refuse to die on command (McCulloch & Till, 2005). The exact properties, the ones that evolution spent hundreds of millions of years building, the ones that are genuinely keeping you alive right now, are the same properties that make cancer not just possible, but inevitable over a long enough timeline

Properties of stem cells were selected for because of their benefits to survival, but the cost is cancer that is probable over a human's lifespan with many accumulated divisions.

That is the case. Not what cancer does but why cancer is the inevitable dark side of the very system that keeps you alive.

Chapter 02

📍 Body's Interior · Stem Cell Districts · 3:15 AM

The Stem Cell District

Every investigation begins with understanding the environment where the crime occurred. In this case, the human body is the environment, a massive, tightly coordinated city of over 37 trillion cells. Most members of this community live short predictable lives. They perform their tasks, divide a few times and eventually retire though programmed cell death.

But not all cells obey the law.

Stem cells operate under their own set of rules. They are the body's permanent repair and maintenance crew, responsible for maintaining organs that constantly experience wear and tear. An example is the intestinal epithelium, which is one of the most rapidly renewing tissues in the body and is replaced approximately every four to five days through the activity of intestinal stem cells. In another case, hematopoietic stem cells in bone marrow continually generate new blood cells to main circulation. Without these regenerative stem cell populations, tissues would quickly deteriorate and fail (Clevers, 2011).

📍 Graphic 1 — Tissue Renewal System

To continuously undergo this workload, stem cells possess abilities that most other cells do not. They are able to divide repeatedly resulting in infinite self-renewal and survive for long periods. Simultaneously, they generate specialised daughter cells that replace damaged or aging tissue. These properties allow tissues to maintain stability across decades and are essential for normal regeneration and repair (Poliwoda et al., 2022).

From an evolutionary perspective, these abilities represent adaptations that were strongly favoured by natural selection. Organisms capable of repairing injury and maintaining internal systems would have had significant survival advantages.

But these privileges come with a cost.

Every time a stem cell divides, its DNA must be copied. Although DNA replication is highly accurate, mutation errors may occur. Over millions of divisions across a lifetime, these errors accumulate and introduce genetic variation amongst cells. While most mutations may be harmless, some affect genes controlling cell growth or survival. When this occurs, a mutated cell may gain a selective advantage over neighboring cells and begin expanding within the tissue environment (Greaves & Maley, 2012).

📍 Graphic 2 — Mutation Accumulation Timeline
Mark — turning point This is where the investigation takes an unexpected turn. The cell responsible for maintaining tissues and keeping the body alive may also provide the conditions under which cancer can emerge. The stem cell, once seen as the body's maintenance worker, now begging to look like a key suspect in the case

As a detective I must wonder. If stem cells are so important for survival, why has evolution not eliminated the mechanism that causes cancer to develop?

Chapter 03

📍 Precinct 37 · Evidence Room · 4:00 AM

The Double Agent

By the case folder, I could already tell this suspect was an extraordinary

Most cells in this city live by the rules of dividing when told, stopping when signaled and die when damaged. However for the case of stem cells they operate under a different set of permissions, with the authority to divide, persist and rebuild. The body relies on these cells to maintain tissues that constantly experience damage or loss, such as intestinal lining and blood system.

This is what makes this particular case complicated.

The same features that make stem cells indispensable also make them dangerous. Rapid proliferation keeps tissues supplied with fresh cells, but every additional division creates another opportunity for replication error. Self renewal prevents the stem pool from being depleted but it also means any mutation that appears in a stem cell can be preserved and copied forward indefinitely. Resistance to cell death protects valuable regenerative cells during stress, yet that same survival capacity can become a liability if the wrong genetic changes accumulate (Reya et al., 2001).

To put it into simpler terms, the system that protects the body simultaneously carries a built in vulnerability.

When mutations occur in long-lived stem cells, they can persist for decades because stem cells have that ability to self renew and continuously generate new daughter cells. This means that a single mutation arising in a stem cell can be inherited by many future cells within the same tissue. Over time, the gradual accumulation of mutations increases the probability that certain cells may behave abnormally and escape normal growth controls.

🗂 Evidence Board — Stem Cell Traits as a Trade-off
Property What It Does For You What It Does To You
Rapid Proliferation Replaces the gut lining every 3–5 days; produces 200 billion blood cells daily; heals wounds fast Each division carries ~1–2 replication errors. More divisions = more errors. More errors = higher cumulative probability of a driver mutation
Self-Renewal Keeps the stem cell pool alive for 70+ years without depleting — essential for lifelong tissue maintenance A mutation in a self-renewing stem cell is immortalised — copied into every daughter cell forever. Unlike a mutation in a short-lived differentiated cell, it never goes away
Apoptosis Resistance Protects stem cells from accidental deletion during stress, famine, or injury — keeps the reserve intact TP53 — the gene that triggers apoptosis in damaged cells — is mutated in over 50% of all human cancers. Cancer cells co-opt the stem cell's death-resistance programme (Vousden & Prives, 2009)
Telomerase Activity Prevents chromosome ends from shortening during repeated divisions — maintains chromosomal stability in long-lived renewing tissues Approximately 85–90% of cancers reactivate telomerase (TERT) to achieve immortality — directly re-deploying a normal stem cell survival mechanism
Epigenetic Plasticity Allows stem cells to respond to tissue demand and differentiate into multiple cell types — enables repair and regeneration Cancer cells exploit this same plasticity to dedifferentiate — reverting to stem-like states with enhanced proliferation and resistance to chemotherapy

The evidence shows that the same system is viewed across different time scales. In the short term, stem cell traits support survival by allowing tissues to regenerate and recover from injury. As mentioned, over decades of repeated divisions, the accumulation of replications of replication errors becomes increasingly likely. Occasionally these mutations affect, genes that regulate cell growth or survival, giving certain cells a small advantage over their neighbors (Greaves & Maley, 2012). Over time, those advantages can accumulate.

Research comparing different tissues has shown that organs with higher numbers of lifetime stem cell divisions tend to show higher cancer incidence. This suggests that normal tissue maintenance itself contributes to the probability that mutations will eventually arise in key regulatory genes.

From an evolutionary perspective, this reflects a fundamental biological compromise. Natural selection favours traits that improve survival and reproductive success early in life. Stem cells play a critical role in this process by allowing organisms to repair damage, regenerative tissues and maintain organ functions across reproductive years. However the same biological traits that provide these benefits may carry long term costs that appear later in life, when natural selection is weaker. This evolutionary compromise is known as antagonistic pleiotropy, a single biological trait provides advantages early in life but produces harmful consequences later. (Aktipis et al., 2015).

📖 Technical Term — Antagonistic Pleiotropy Stem cell biology fits this pattern well. The capacity for self-renewal, rapid proliferation and long term persistence allows tissues to function across decades. Yet those same properties create the condition under which cancer can eventually emerge. The genes associated with these processes tell the same story. The body did not evolve stem cells to cause disease but instead cancer emerged as an unintended consequence of biological systems that were selected because they help organisms survive. The benefits of these traits appear immediately: tissues can regenerate, wounds can heal, and organs can continue functioning. The costs may only become visible decades later. This is the trade-off that multicellular life accepts.
Mark The genes associated with these processes tell the same story. One of the most researched examples is TP53, often described as the "guardian of the genome". Its function is to help detect DNA damage and trigger apoptosis when cells become too damaged to repair. TP53 is the most frequently mutated gene in human cancers, with mutation occurring in more than half of all tumours.

Chapter 04

📍 Precinct 37 · Cold Case Archive · 5:30 AM

The Long Con

Every detective knows: the best cons take time. The mark doesn't feel the loss until long after the deal was made. Evolution pulled the longest con in biological history — and the terms were set before the first human being ever existed.

Here's how it worked. For most of human evolutionary history, lifespans were short. Predation, infection, starvation — the original crime syndicate of the ancestral environment — kept most people from living long past their reproductive years. Evolution, being the ruthless accountant that it is, invested heavily in the early years: strong tissue repair, fast cell division, active telomerase. The bill? Paid later. Much later.

This is what evolutionary biologists call antagonistic pleiotropy — when a single gene or trait provides a benefit early in life and extracts a cost late in life. Selection is nearly blind to late-life costs, especially when most ancestral individuals never survived to pay them.

📖 Technical Term: Antagonistic Pleiotropy Antagonistic pleiotropy — when a single gene has multiple, opposing effects on fitness at different life stages. A gene that enhances reproduction at age 20 will be strongly maintained by natural selection even if it increases cancer risk at age 60. The early benefit is "visible" to evolution; the late cost is largely invisible, especially when most ancestral humans didn't survive long past reproductive age anyway. Plain language: evolution paid for the stem cell system on credit. The benefits came up front. The cancer bill arrives decades later. By then, reproduction is already done. Evolution doesn't care about the bill.

I pulled the mathematics on this. If you want to understand why cancer happens, you have to understand what happens inside a single stem cell lineage over a human lifetime.

Cancer is not primarily a story of things going catastrophically wrong. It is the predictable statistical output of a system that must divide thousands of times to keep you alive, and cannot do so without accumulating errors. The long con, expressed in mathematics.

The correlation between total lifetime stem cell divisions and cancer incidence, across 31 different tissue types — r² = 0.804. The colon, dividing 73 times a year, has among the highest cancer rates of any organ. The small intestine, right next door, facing the same chemical environment, divides far less frequently and has dramatically lower cancer rates. 📍 Body's Interior · Tissue Districts · Underground · Decades in the Making

Evolution's Black Market

In this case, we discover that the body has been running its own kind of underground evolution-quietly unfolding for decades.

I have been on this beat long enough to know that crime doesn't appear out of nowhere. It organizes itself. It evolves. A mutation that starts as a minor bookkeeping error becomes, twenty years later, becomes the operation controlling the entire district.

Cancer works in much the same way. It doesn't emerge all at once,it develops gradually. It is a process– Darwinian evolutionary process,taking place within the tissue of a single organism, often over many years or even decades.

And for evolution to run,three ingredients are required. When I checked the file, the tumour had all three.

🗂 Evidence File — Darwin's Three Conditions, Met Inside the Body

1. Variation: Every stem cell division introduces somatic mutations — small genetic differences between cells. No two cells in an aged stem cell compartment are genetically identical. The raw material for selection is continuously generated.

2. Heritability: When a stem cell divides, its mutations are passed to daughter cells. An advantageous mutation is not a one-time event — it propagates through the entire descendant lineage. Traits are heritable at the cellular level.

3. Differential Reproduction: A stem cell that acquires a mutation giving it a growth advantage — faster division, resistance to apoptosis, evasion of tissue growth limits — will outcompete its neighbours. Its lineage expands. In a fixed tissue space, expansion means competition. The fittest lineage wins.

A tumour is not a uniform mass of identical abnormal cells. Rather, it represents a population–a Darwinian ecosystem characterized by genetic diversity and ongoing selection pressure. Different regions of the tumour often carry distinct mutation profiles, while subclonal populations compete within the tumour microenvironment. Cells with advantageous traits outcompete others and dominate the population.

Even a small growth advantage compounds dramatically over cellular generations. Small consistent advantages become dominant clones over months to years. This is why cancer is primarily associated with aging. Somatic evolution takes time and long lived humans provide the time necessary for these evolutionary processes to unfold (Alberts et al., 2002).

📐 Evidence Exhibit B — The Selection Equation
p(t+1) = p(t) × (1 + s) / [ p(t) × (1 + s) + (1 − p(t)) ] // p(t) = frequency of the mutant clone at time t // s = selective advantage over normal cells // (s = 0.10 means 10% faster net growth per generation) // Example: p₀ = 0.01 (starts at 1% of the tissue), s = 0.10 Gen 1: p ≈ 0.0110 | Gen 2: p ≈ 0.0121 Gen 3: p ≈ 0.0133 | Gen 5: p ≈ 0.0160 Gen 10: p ≈ 0.0249 | Gen 20: p ≈ 0.0585 Gen 50: p ≈ 0.311 | Gen 70: p ≈ 0.621 // Small consistent advantages become dominant clones over months to years. // This is why cancer is primarily a disease of ageing — somatic evolution // needs time. Long-lived humans provide exactly that.
Plain language: A mutant stem cell with even a tiny growth advantage is like a business that makes 10% more profit than its competitors every cycle. Month to month you barely notice. Year to year, it takes over the market. Decades later, it owns the district. The maths is patient. The biology is not on your side.

The Treatment Problem

Here is what makes this case particularly hard to close. Treatment becomes part of the crime scene.

Chemotherapy then acts as a strong selective pressure on this evolving population. A drug that kills 95% of a tumour does not end the evolutionary process- it changes it. The majority of drug sensitive cells die, but that 5% of cells that may carry pre-existing resistance mutations. Once the sensitive cells are removed, the resistant cells suddenly have access to more space, nutrients with less competition. These surviving cells then expand and undergo clonal selection. Over time, the resistant clones can become the dominant population, demonstrating evolution in action. This is why treatment can unintentionally drive the selection of drug-resistant cancer cells and lead to tumour recurrence (O'Brien et al., 2006).

Mark The cancer didn't develop resistance. It was selected for it. The resistant subclone was always there, a minor variant in the tumour population, waiting for the right conditions to expand. That is Darwin's racket — turn your treatment strategy against you.

This is why the most promising new approach– adaptive therapy, doesn't aim to eradicate the tumour completely. Instead, they focus on managing it, keeping a stable population of treatment-sensitive cells alive so that they continue to compete with resistant clones. By doing this, tumour's own evolutionary dynamics will be a weapon against it (Gatenby et al., 2009).

🔬 Course Connection — Somatic Evolution and Population Genetics The somatic evolution framework applies core population genetics concepts at the cellular level. Clonal expansion mirrors a selective sweep. The selection coefficient (s) quantifies cellular fitness advantage exactly as it does organismal fitness. Clonal haematopoiesis — detectable expansion of mutant blood stem cell clones in 10–20% of people over age 70 — is direct empirical evidence of Darwinian selection occurring within a living human body, observable by modern sequencing. Mutation, selection, drift, and expansion: the same forces operating at a different biological level.

📍 Precinct 37 · Briefing Room · 9:00 AM

Conflicting Testimony

No case is totally clean. Witnesses contradict each other as evidence can point two ways at once. A good detective views both contradictions and doesn't ignore them.

Here are the open disputes on the file.

⚖️ Dispute 01 — Bad Luck vs. Lifestyle: How Much of Cancer Risk Comes from Intrinsic Cell Division vs. the Environment?
🟢 Witness A — The Intrinsic Division View Back in 2015, Tomasetti and Vogelstein compared cancer incidence across many tissue types and found something striking, that tissues that undergo more lifetime stem cell divisions tend to have higher cancer rates regardless of known environmental exposures (Tomasetti & Vogelstein, 2015). The majority of driver mutations don't arise from environmental mutagens but from unavoidable replication errors. Every time a stem cell divides, it must copy its DNA. Copying DNA introduces occasional replication errors and over decades of tissue maintenance these errors accumulate. In this view, many cancers don't primarily arise from external exposures but just from the unavoidable mathematics of DNA replication. So, the price of maintaining living tissues contributes to some fraction of cancer risk (Tomasetti, Li, & Vogelstein, 2017).
🔴 Witness B — The Environment Matters View The "bad luck" framing was criticized for not appropriately weighing environmental and lifestyle contributions especially for cancers that have well established environmental causes such as lung cancer, hepatocellular carcinoma, or skin cancer (Couzin-Frankel, 2015). Factors such as tobacco, UV radiation, chronic infection, and diet can amplify the baseline mutation rate beyond what stem cell divisions alone would produce (Song & Giovannucci, 2016; Wu et al., 2016). So, by calling it bad luck, it takes away from the actual preventative value of lifestyle and policy interventions.
⚖ Mark's Assessment — Both witnesses are telling the truth about different parts of the same case. Intrinsic replication errors set a baseline cancer risk that exists even in the absence of any environmental exposure. Environmental factors act to elevate that baseline.
⚖️ Dispute 02 — Does a Boss Cell Run the Tumour? The Cancer Stem Cell Debate
🟢 Witness A — The Hierarchy Model According to the cancer stem cell (CSC) model, tumours are organized like a hierarchy. A small subpopulation of cancer stem cells that are identified by markers like CD44+/CD24 in breast cancer, and CD133+ in glioblastoma maintain the tumour, drive regrowth after treatment, and are responsible for metastasis (Al-Hajj et al., 2003; Singh et al., 2003). By taking out CSC subpopulation, you take out the majority of the cancer operation. Strong evidence in leukemia, glioblastoma, and breast cancer supports the hierarchy model (Lapidot et al., 1994).
🔴 Witness B — The Plasticity Model Other researchers argue that the hierarchy is not fixed. Under stress, especially after chemotherapy ordinary tumor cells can dedifferentiate and revert to a stem cell like state. CSC identity is a dynamic state and not a defined subpopulation. Therefore, by targeting surface markers alone won't eliminate the operation because the other remaining cells can re acquire stem like properties. So, the hierarchy exists but it is fluid (Gupta et al., 2011).
⚖ Mark's Assessment — Evidence suggests that both models are operating. The hierarchy and plasticity models aren't mutually exclusive. What matters more depends on the type of tumor and the clinical context. More cancer biology research is needed, therefore the case is still open.
⚖️ Dispute 03 — Is Antagonistic Pleiotropy the Right Framework for Cancer?
🟢 Witness A — The Tradeoff Framework Supporters of the antagonistic pleiotropy argue that stem cell properties are actively maintained by selection throughout reproductive life and are not neutral post reproductive drift (Williams, 1957). Cancer affects reproductive age individuals too so the cost which is cancer is not exclusively post reproductive. Rapid division and self renewal keep tissues functioning and organisms alive long enough to reproduce so these traist help to provide early life benefits. The downside which is cancer risk is tied to these same properties therefore, cancer represents an evolutionary tradeoff as it has benefits early in life and costs later (Ungewitter & Scrable, 2008).
🔴 Witness B — The Neutral Drift Argument Others argue that the story may be simpler. If most cancer mortality occurs after reproductive age then natural selection pressure against cancer enabling stem cells properties is very weak (Medawar, 1952). Under this model, the relationship between stem cells and cancer may not be a true evolutionary tradeoff but more of a case of neutral late life consequences of traits that selection simply can't see well enough to modify and just didn't have a strong reason to eliminate (Austad & Hoffman, 2018).
⚖ Mark's Assessment — This one remains an open question and is a legitimate theoretical dispute. Some aspects of cancer risk reflect evolutionary tradeoffs whereas others may be closer to neutral late life consequences of traits that were favored by evolution for different reasons entirely. The distinction mainly matters for how we theorise about the evolutionary origins of cancer. So, the case is far from closed.
Mark I've been in enough briefing rooms to know that the most dangerous thing you can do is walk out of one thinking you've got the full picture. These disputes aren't weaknesses in the case. They're the honest state of knowledge. Any version of this story that pretends the science is settled is lying to you — and a good detective never lies, even when the truth is inconvenient.

Q2 Conceptual
Why does cancer incidence increase sharply with age, even without specific environmental mutagen exposure? What does this tell us about stem cell biology and the evolutionary tradeoff?

Q3 Critical Thinking
A classmate argues: "Cancer is just random bad luck — somatic mutations happen by accident, there's nothing evolutionary about it. Evolution requires heritability across generations, and somatic mutations don't pass to offspring." Construct a detailed rebuttal using the somatic evolution framework.
▾
A strong rebuttal addresses three points:

1. Evolution does not require germline heritability to occur. The three requirements of Darwinian evolution are variation, heritability, and differential fitness. Somatic mutations satisfy all three at the cellular level — they generate genetic variation between cells, daughter cells inherit mutations from parent cells (heritable within the organismal lifespan), and mutations conferring growth advantage increase in frequency via selection. The evolutionary process is valid regardless of whether mutations cross generational lines.

2. "Bad luck" is not non-evolutionary — it is the mechanistic substrate of the tradeoff. The "bad luck" framing and the evolutionary tradeoff framing are complementary, not contradictory. The reason stem cells accumulate replication errors at the rate they do is because natural selection shaped them to divide rapidly and self-renew. Selection built the system that makes "bad luck" statistically probable. The probability of cancer is a predictable mathematical output of evolved stem cell properties — not a random departure from them.

3. Selection demonstrably acts on somatic variants in real time. Clonal haematopoiesis — the age-related expansion of mutant blood stem cell clones detectable in 10–20% of people over 70 — is direct empirical evidence of Darwinian selection operating within living human bodies right now, with no germline transmission involved. The selection coefficient, clonal sweep dynamics, and fitness advantages of specific driver mutations (DNMT3A, TET2, ASXL1) have all been quantified using population genetics tools applied to somatic data. This is evolution. It just happens to be inside rather than between organisms.
Q4 Math
A haematopoietic stem cell (HSC) divides ~13 times/year. Somatic mutation rate: 1.3 × 10⁻⁷ per base pair per division. Coding genome: 3.2 × 10⁷ base pairs. Calculate: (a) coding mutations per HSC division, and (b) total coding mutations per HSC lineage over 60 years. Using the selection equation p(t+1) = p(t)(1+s) / [p(t)(1+s) + (1−p(t))], calculate clone frequency after 5 generations for p₀ = 0.01, s = 0.10.

Part (a) — Mutations per division:
1.3 × 10⁻⁷ mutations/bp × 3.2 × 10⁷ bp = 4.16 mutations per division

Part (b) — Total over 60 years:
13 divisions/year × 60 years = 780 total divisions
780 × 4.16 ≈ ~3,245 coding mutations per HSC lineage

Selection equation — 5 generations, p₀ = 0.01, s = 0.10:
Gen 1: (0.01 × 1.10) / (0.01 × 1.10 + 0.99) = 0.011 / 1.001 ≈ 0.01099
Gen 2: (0.01099 × 1.10) / (0.01099 × 1.10 + 0.98901) ≈ 0.01209
Gen 3: ≈ 0.01329 | Gen 4: ≈ 0.01461 | Gen 5: ≈ 0.01604

Interpretation: A mutant HSC clone starting at 1% of the tissue with a 10% selective advantage reaches ~1.6% after just 5 cellular generations — modest but compounding. Over hundreds of generations spanning months to years, this becomes dominant clonal expansion. This mathematics is the mechanism behind clonal haematopoiesis and the stepwise progression of leukaemia. The tradeoff between haematopoietic maintenance and accumulated mutation risk is not limited to high-turnover tissues — it is a universal feature of any stem cell system that must operate across decades.
Q5 Scenario
A patient inherits one mutated BRCA1 copy (heterozygous germline mutation). Using the somatic evolution framework and antagonistic pleiotropy, explain their elevated breast cancer risk. Address: (a) BRCA1's normal role in stem cells, (b) what the "second hit" is and why it matters, and (c) how this illustrates the course theme of adaptations carrying disadvantages under certain circumstances.
▾
(a) BRCA1's normal role in stem cells:
BRCA1 encodes a tumour suppressor protein essential for homologous recombination — the high-fidelity pathway that repairs double-strand DNA breaks during replication. In mammary stem cells, which undergo significant proliferation during puberty, pregnancy, and across reproductive life, BRCA1 ensures that replication errors are accurately repaired rather than becoming heritable somatic mutations. Without functional BRCA1, replication errors accumulate at an accelerated rate — the mutation burden per division increases, compressing the somatic evolutionary timeline toward cancer.

(b) The "second hit" — Knudson's hypothesis:
With one germline mutant BRCA1 copy, every cell in the body starts life already halfway to BRCA1 loss-of-function. The "second hit" is a somatic mutation in the remaining functional allele, arising in a mammary stem cell during normal tissue maintenance. Once both copies are inactivated, DNA double-strand breaks go unrepaired — subsequent replication errors accumulate uncorrected, exponentially accelerating the mutation burden. The germline mutation is a head start in the somatic evolutionary race: far fewer total divisions are needed to reach the threshold of driver mutations required for malignancy.

(c) Antagonistic pleiotropy and the course theme:
This case illustrates the theme at two levels. First, the underlying tradeoff: mammary stem cells must divide extensively to support reproductive function — a strong fitness benefit — and this division requires efficient DNA repair. When repair is compromised, the cost of the adaptation is dramatically amplified. Second, the BRCA1 germline mutation itself may have been maintained in some populations because heterozygous carriers show reproductive advantages in specific contexts — a possible antagonistic pleiotropy at the population level, where the cancer cost is post-reproductive and therefore partially invisible to selection. The adaptation (stem cell proliferation for reproductive function) carries its disadvantage (cancer) most visibly under the circumstances of a long modern lifespan, directly illustrating the course's central theme.
